%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
%   ABSTRACT
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\Abstract{
We present a retrieval-augmented AI assistant for Erasmus+ exchange
support at UL FRI, retrieving information from curated official sources including university materials, Erasmus+ guidance documents,
and resources obtained directly through the UL FRI Erasmus
coordination process. Documents are chunked, embedded using a
sentence-transformer model, indexed with FAISS, and retrieved using
Maximum Marginal Relevance search before answer generation with local
open-weight language models. The system is evaluated on a manually
curated benchmark of 210 Erasmus-related questions using three
complementary approaches: BERTScore semantic evaluation, LLM-as-judge
assessment via the Gemini API, and manual human evaluation, comparing
two local open-weight models, Llama 3.1-8B and Gemma 3-4B, under
initial ($k=3$) and tuned ($k=7$) retrieval configurations. Results
show that retrieval grounding reduces hallucinations by nearly an order
of magnitude compared to a Gemini no-retrieval baseline, with manual
evaluation scores improving from 1.88 to 2.32 for the best RAG
configuration, and that retrieval depth matters more than model scale
in this domain.
}

\section*{Introduction}

Students preparing for Erasmus exchange at UL FRI must navigate a
large amount of administrative information spread across faculty pages,
University of Ljubljana documents, official Erasmus+ guidance, PDF
forms, and partner university catalogues. Questions are often
procedural and context-dependent, covering deadlines, Learning
Agreements, ECTS recognition, financial support, visa requirements, and
post-mobility documentation, and rarely have a single authoritative
source. General-purpose language models can produce fluent and plausible sounding
answers, but without access to institution-specific sources they often
hallucinate deadlines, funding amounts, or eligibility conditions. In an
administrative context, such errors can directly harm a student's exchange
plans.

We address this by developing a domain-specific AI assistant grounded
in curated official and institution-specific sources.Our approach is based on retrieval-augmented generation (RAG): the
system retrieves relevant document chunks from a curated Erasmus
knowledge base and uses a large language model to generate an answer
grounded in the retrieved content. This combines the flexibility of conversational question
answering with the factual grounding of official documents, reducing
the risk of unsupported or hallucinated responses.

We evaluate whether retrieval grounding improves factual reliability
compared to a Gemini 2.5 Pro no-retrieval baseline, and compare two local
open-weight models, Llama 3.1-8B and Gemma 3-4B, under both an
initial retrieval configuration ($k{=}3$) and a tuned one ($k{=}7$).

\section*{Dataset}

Since no pre-existing dataset exists for this domain, we constructed
our own knowledge base from publicly available institutional and
Erasmus-related sources. The dataset combines publicly available
university materials with Erasmus-specific resources obtained through
direct interaction with the UL FRI Erasmus coordination process.
Institutional sources include the UL FRI website and the University of
Ljubljana central website. We additionally incorporated
official Erasmus+ guidance documents covering financial support, Online
Learning Agreements (OLA), student rights and obligations, visa and
residence procedures, doctoral mobility, traineeships, and UL FRI
partner institutions. Structured course-guidance files for selected
partner universities were also prepared to support questions related to
course selection and exchange planning.

A particularly valuable part of the dataset was obtained through direct
engagement with the Erasmus coordination process at UL FRI. We attended
the official Erasmus exchange kickoff meeting for the 2026/27 academic
year, where we interacted with the main coordinator, Vesna Gračner, and
obtained the official presentation used during the session. We also
systematically recorded and transcribed student questions raised during
the Q\&A session, together with the coordinator's answers. These
question-answer pairs capture realistic information needs and practical
procedural clarifications that are not consistently documented in
official written materials. The collected materials were organised by
topic, covering admissions, study programmes, visa and residence
procedures, student life, and Erasmus exchange. Additionally the Q\&A content
was used both to enrich the knowledge base and to construct the
evaluation benchmark described in the Results section.

\section*{Methods}

The assistant is built around a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)
pipeline consisting of two stages: an offline indexing stage that
preprocesses and stores the knowledge base, and an online inference
stage that retrieves relevant passages and generates a grounded answer. The overall architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:rag-pipeline}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig/rag_architecture.pdf}
    \caption{Overview of the RAG pipeline. The offline phase builds a
    FAISS vector index from the curated knowledge base; the online phase
    retrieves relevant chunks and generates a grounded answer.}
    \label{fig:rag-pipeline}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{Document indexing}

All knowledge-base documents are loaded and parsed before any queries
are processed. Plain text files are read directly, while PDF documents
are parsed page by page. 

Documents are split into overlapping chunks using a recursive character
splitter. We experimented with chunk sizes of 300, 500, and 700
characters and found that 500 characters with a 50-character overlap
offered the best balance between retrieval precision and contextual
completeness. Smaller chunks frequently fragmented multi-step procedural
answers, while larger chunks introduced irrelevant context. Each chunk
is embedded into a dense vector representation using the
\textit{all-MiniLM-L6-v2} sentence-transformer
model~\cite{reimers2019sentencebert}, with output vectors L2-normalised
to unit length. The resulting embeddings are stored in a FAISS flat
index~\cite{johnson2019faiss}, saved to disk and reused at query time.

\subsection*{Retrieval and generation}

At query time, the FAISS index is used as the backbone of a retriever
configured with Maximum Marginal Relevance (MMR) search. MMR selects
chunks that are both relevant to the query and mutually diverse,
reducing redundancy when multiple passages cover the same topic.
We tuned the retrieval configuration by varying the number of retrieved
chunks $k$ (3, 5, 7), chunk size (300, 500, 700 characters), search
type (MMR vs.\ similarity), and embedding model (MiniLM vs.\ mpnet),
evaluating each combination on 50 questions. As shown in
Table~\ref{tab:tuning}, $k{=}7$ with MMR consistently produced the
highest F1 and key-information coverage with the lowest fallback rate,
and was adopted as the final configuration. In the MMR setup, 20
candidate chunks are initially fetched (fetch\_k$=20$), the top 7 are
selected by balancing relevance and diversity ($\lambda{=}0.6$, where
higher values favour relevance over diversity). Both the baseline
($k{=}3$) and tuned ($k{=}7$) configurations were evaluated on the
full 210-question benchmark to quantify the impact of retrieval depth.

The retrieved chunks are concatenated into a context string, each
prefixed with its source path, and injected into a structured prompt
that instructs the model to answer using only the provided passages.
If the context does not contain a sufficient answer, the model responds
with a fixed fallback message directing the student to the UL FRI
website or student office, rather than generating an unsupported
response.

Answer generation is handled locally on the ARNES HPC cluster using
two open-weight models: \textit{Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct} and
\textit{Gemma 3-4B-IT}. Llama 3.1-8B served as the primary model and consistently outperformed
Gemma 3-4B on questions requiring exact procedural or administrative
details specific to UL FRI.
The full pipeline is assembled as a single LangChain LCEL chain: the
retriever feeds formatted context into the prompt template, which is
passed to the language model and parsed into plain text answer.



\section*{Results}
We evaluated the system on a manually curated benchmark of 210
Erasmus-related questions covering procedural, administrative, legal,
and general informational topics. Questions were constructed to span
varying levels of retrieval dependence, from those requiring exact
institutional information to broader questions that can be partially
answered from general world knowledge. The benchmark also includes
deliberately vague and ambiguous cases to reflect realistic student
interactions.

Two models were evaluated within the same RAG pipeline: Llama 3.1-8B
and Gemma 3-4B, each under an initial retrieval configuration ($k=3$)
and the tuned configuration ($k=7$).

\subsection*{BERTScore evaluation}

We tested 14 different BERT score metrics, of the families DeBERTa v1 \cite{he2021deberta}, DeBERTa v2 \cite{he2021debertav2_hf}, DeBERTa v3 \cite{he2021debertav3}, XLNet \cite{yang2019xlnet}, ELECTRA \cite{clark2020electra} and RoBERTa \cite{liu2019roberta} using bootstrap settings: 10,000 resamples, 95\% percentile confidence intervals, seed 42. The highest absolute-F1 scorer across all model configurations was roberta-large with mean F1 0.8787. On the other hand, the lowest absolute-F1 scorer was xlnet-large-cased with mean F1 0.5541. We report results for the best-performing scorer, roberta-large,
as shown in Table~\ref{tab:tuned_vs_not_tuned}. Full results for
all 14 configurations are available in the project repository.

\subsection*{LLM-as-judge evaluation}

We used the Gemini API to evaluate how well the five models answered each of the 210 questions. The results in Table~\ref{tab:model_performance} indicate a noticeable bias toward the Gemini baseline responses. This is likely because the Gemini baseline system prompt explicitly included the university websites \href{https://www.uni-lj.si/}{uni-lj.si} and \href{https://fri.uni-lj.si/}{fri.uni-lj.si}, providing the model with direct access to some available information. The Gemini-generated answers frequently referenced links and documents that appeared plausible and contextually appropriate at first glance. As a result, the evaluation process often failed to distinguish between superficially convincing answers and genuinely correct ones, which likely contributed to inflated evaluation scores compared to the other models.

The Gemini API evaluation system assigns scores in four bands: Correct, Mostly correct, Partially correct, and Incorrect.

\subsection*{Human evaluation}

While automated metrics capture lexical and semantic similarity,
they cannot reliably detect subtle hallucinations or assess whether
an answer is practically useful for a student. We therefore conducted
a manual evaluation using a four-level scoring scheme, in which \textbf{3} indicates a correct and complete answer, \textbf{2} indicates a mostly correct answer but missing important details, \textbf{1} indicates a partially correct or vague answer, and \textbf{0} indicates an incorrect or unusable answer.

During manual evaluation, responses were additionally annotated for hallucinations. A hallucination was defined as any unsupported or fabricated information generated by the model, either as part of an otherwise correct answer or as the entire response. Fallback responses indicating that sufficient information was unavailable and directing students to the relevant office were not considered hallucinations and could still receive positive scores if the fallback was appropriate for the question.

The Gemini baseline was first evaluated automatically using an LLM-as-a-judge approach, but the scores appeared overly optimistic and did not sufficiently penalize unsupported procedural claims or subtle hallucinations.We therefore manually re-evaluated the baseline using the same protocol applied to all RAG configurations. This reduced the baseline average score from 2.09 to 1.88 and decreased the proportion of fully correct answers from 55.7\% to 45.2\%. Notably, many hallucinations were not detected automatically because the model failed to recognize outdated or institution-specific information.

Table~\ref{tab:manual-eval-overall} indicates that retrieval primarily improved factual grounding and answer reliability rather than only raw correctness scores. Although the baseline often produced fluent and detailed answers for broader informational questions, it was frequently overconfident when handling vague or institution-specific procedures. This was especially visible when information differed across multiple partially outdated sources, such as Erasmus deadlines, travel support amounts, or administrative requirements. In several cases, the model hallucinated non-existent links or mixed outdated and current procedural information within the same answer.

Importantly, many baseline answers that appeared mostly correct still included additional unsupported details that could confuse students or contradict official procedures. In contrast, retrieval grounding reduced hallucination rates by nearly an order of magnitude across all evaluated configurations.
The results also suggest that retrieval depth had a stronger impact than the choice of local language model. Increasing the number of retrieved chunks from $k=3$ to $k=7$ consistently improved both Llama and Gemma, indicating that broader contextual coverage helped the models recover important procedural details and constraints that were often omitted in the smaller retrieval setup.

Interestingly, the tuned Gemma configuration approached the performance of the larger Llama model despite having fewer parameters, suggesting that retrieval quality and context selection may play a more important role in this domain than model scale alone.

Figure~\ref{fig:per-category-results} shows that retrieval-dependent questions benefit the most from increasing retrieval depth. Both models produce more fully correct answers (score 3) and fewer failures (score 0) in the \textit{required} category after increasing retrieval from $k=3$ to $k=7$. This suggests that additional retrieved context primarily improves access to institution-specific procedural details that are difficult to answer from parametric model knowledge alone.

The effect is particularly pronounced for Gemma 3-4B, whose performance gap relative to Llama 3.1-8B becomes noticeably smaller after tuning. This indicates that smaller models are more sensitive to retrieval quality and context coverage.

In contrast, questions categorized as \textit{not needed} show smaller differences between retrieval configurations, suggesting that broader informational questions depend less on retrieval grounding and more on the underlying language model itself.

\section*{Discussion}

Base models often produced plausible high-level answers, while
retrieval grounding helped recover institution-specific details
necessary for practical student guidance. Automatic metrics alone
were insufficient to capture this difference, as they rewarded
semantically similar but procedurally unreliable answers, making
manual evaluation essential for assessing factual grounding and
hallucinations accurately.

Most observed errors were not completely incorrect answers, but
plausible yet incomplete responses missing deadlines, eligibility
constraints, or procedural details specific to UL FRI. In
retrieval-grounded configurations, errors were typically caused by
ambiguous questions, context-dependent scenarios where the correct
answer depends on the student's specific situation, or cases not
explicitly covered in the knowledge base, often resulting in
appropriate fallback responses.

The results suggest that smaller local models can achieve competitive
performance when paired with sufficiently strong retrieval. Despite
the substantial difference in model size, the tuned Gemma
configuration approached the performance of the larger Llama model
after retrieval depth was increased, indicating that careful retrieval
design may provide larger practical gains than model scale alone in
closed institutional domains. Future work could extend the knowledge base with automatically
scraped and updated sources, introduce a mechanism for detecting
and flagging outdated information such as expired deadlines or
changed funding amounts, and explore re-ranking or query expansion
to further improve retrieval precision.
